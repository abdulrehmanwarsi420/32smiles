Dental Trauma


Dental trauma is any injury to your teeth, gums, jawbone, or soft tissues of your mouth. Accidents, such as falls, car wrecks, and sports-related injuries are the main cause of traumatic dental injuries. Early treatment gives you the best chance for full recovery.


Overview


Dental trauma refers to any mouth injury. This includes injury to your teeth, gums, jaws, tongue, lips, or surrounding structures.

Traumatic dental injuries range from mild to severe. Some are dental emergencies and require immediate treatment.


If you have a severe dental injury, call your dentist right away. If your dentist isn’t available, head to the nearest emergency room.


Dental Trauma Types


Common types of dental injuries include:


  • Chipped teeth

  • Cracked teeth

  • Dislodged teeth

  • Knocked-out teeth

  • Cuts in your soft tissue

  • Broken or dislocated jaw

What to Do if You Have Dental Trauma


Call a dentist immediately for instructions. If you don’t have a dentist, visit an urgent care clinic.


Meanwhile, you can:


  • Locate broken pieces of a fractured tooth and bring them to your appointment.

  • If a tooth is knocked out, rinse it and try to place it back in its socket.

  • Use over-the-counter pain relievers for pain and an ice pack for swelling.


Symptoms of Dental Trauma


The symptoms of dental trauma depend on the severity of the injury. Common symptoms include:


  • Toothache or severe pain in the affected area

  • Bleeding from the gums or mouth

  • Swelling around the injured area

  • Loose or missing teeth

  • Visible damage to the tooth (chips, cracks, etc.)

Causes of Dental Trauma


Dental trauma can result from various incidents, including:


  • Sports injuries, especially contact sports

  • Falls or accidents

  • Car accidents

  • Fights or physical altercations

  • Biting on hard objects

Precautions to Prevent Dental Trauma


Preventing dental trauma involves taking certain precautions, such as:


  • Wearing mouthguards during sports

  • Avoiding biting on hard objects like ice or pens

  • Using seat belts while driving

  • Childproofing your home to prevent accidents

Treatment for Dental Trauma


Treatment for dental trauma varies depending on the type and severity of the injury:


  • For chipped or cracked teeth, your dentist might recommend dental bonding or crowns.

  • Dislodged or knocked-out teeth may need to be repositioned or replaced.

  • Soft tissue injuries might require stitches or medication to control pain and swelling.

  • Broken jaws or severe injuries may need surgical intervention.
