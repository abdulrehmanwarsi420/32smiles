Impacted Wisdom Teeth


Impacted wisdom teeth happen when your third molars become partially or fully trapped in your gums or jawbone. This can lead to a number of oral health problems, including infection, tooth decay, and gum disease. Surgical removal is often recommended as treatment for impacted wisdom teeth.


Overview


What are impacted wisdom teeth?


Wisdom teeth — also called third molars — are the last set of teeth that grow in. They usually erupt (break through your gums) between the ages of 17 and 25. Sometimes, wisdom teeth erupt in alignment with your other teeth and don’t cause any problems. Other times, they become either partially or fully trapped in your gums or jawbone. This is referred to as impaction. Impacted wisdom teeth can cause a variety of oral health problems, including cavities, gum disease, and infection.


Fully impacted wisdom teeth, partially impacted wisdom teeth, and non-impacted wisdom teeth: What’s the difference?


Fully impacted wisdom teeth aren’t visible. They’re completely hidden underneath your gums. A partially impacted wisdom tooth is slightly visible because part of it has erupted. Non-impacted wisdom teeth have erupted and are completely visible above your gum line. It’s important to note that non-impacted wisdom teeth can still cause problems.


Soft tissue impaction vs. hard tissue impaction


You might hear your dentist use the terms “soft tissue impaction” and “hard tissue impaction.” Soft tissue impaction means your tooth has erupted from your jawbone but hasn’t broken through your gums. Hard tissue impaction means your tooth is still completely covered by your gums and jawbone.


What are the different types of impacted wisdom teeth?


There are several types of impacted wisdom teeth. Each type matches with the positioning of your tooth:


  • Mesiangular: This type occurs when your wisdom tooth is angled toward the front of your mouth.

  • Distoangular: This type happens when your wisdom tooth is angled toward the back of your mouth.

  • Vertical: This occurs when your wisdom tooth is in the correct position for eruption, but it’s still trapped beneath your gums.

  • Horizontal: This happens when your wisdom tooth is lying completely on its side, trapped beneath your gums. Horizontally impacted wisdom teeth are often painful because they place excess pressure on the teeth in front of them.

  • Buccoangular: This type occurs when your wisdom tooth is angled toward the cheek (buccal side) and the angle between the tooth and the jaw is acute.

  • Linguangular: This occurs when the tooth is angled toward the tongue (lingual side) with an acute angle.

  • Inverted: This type is rare and occurs when the wisdom tooth is rotated 180 degrees and the root is pointing toward the jawbone.

Causes of Impacted Wisdom Teeth


Impacted wisdom teeth are typically caused by a lack of space in the jaw to accommodate these new molars. Genetics can play a role, as jaw size is often inherited. If your jaw is too small to allow wisdom teeth to erupt properly, they may become impacted. Additionally, delayed or abnormal tooth eruption patterns can contribute to impaction.


Care and Prevention


Good oral hygiene is essential to minimize the risk of problems associated with impacted wisdom teeth. Regular dental checkups can help monitor the development of your wisdom teeth. Dentists may recommend dental X-rays to assess their position and decide if removal is necessary. Maintaining proper brushing and flossing techniques can also help prevent infections around impacted teeth.


Treatment Options


The most common treatment for impacted wisdom teeth is surgical removal, also known as extraction. The procedure is typically performed by an oral surgeon under local or general anesthesia. After the extraction, proper post-operative care is essential to ensure smooth healing and to prevent complications like infection or dry socket. Your dentist or oral surgeon will provide specific instructions for recovery, including managing pain and swelling, maintaining a soft diet, and avoiding strenuous activity.


In some cases, if the wisdom teeth are not causing any symptoms or problems, they may not need to be removed. However, regular monitoring is necessary to ensure that they do not start to cause issues over time.
